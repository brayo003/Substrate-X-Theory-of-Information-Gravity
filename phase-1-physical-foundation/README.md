## 1. The Three Effective Laws of Substrate X

These laws describe the effective, coarse-grained behavior of information dynamics in Substrate X.
They are not claimed as ultimate microscopic laws.

### 1st Law: Conservation of Information–Energy (Source Law)

Definition:
Information density s is not free; it is generated only through coupling to the energy density E and force density F of the environment.

Equation Link:
αE + γF

Operational Meaning:
Information cannot increase without input.
If E = 0 and F = 0, information density must decay.

### 2nd Law: Irreversibility and Decay (Entropy Law)

Definition:
In any transport process, a portion of information density is permanently lost to the substrate.

Equation Link:
−σ_irr

Operational Meaning:
Information has a finite lifetime.
Without sustained excitation, entropy reduces system tension or focus toward zero.
Perfect memory is physically impossible.

### 3rd Law: Causal Speed Limit (Inertia Law)

Definition:
Information cannot propagate or react instantaneously; its dynamics are bounded by the damping time τ and the causal speed limit c.

Equation Link:
τ ∂²s / ∂t² (Telegrapher term)

Operational Meaning:
There exists a mandatory warm-up time for any transition.
Instantaneous jumps to high-focus or high-certainty states are forbidden.

## 2. Fundamental Assumptions and Validity Limits

All Phase-1 derivations, simulations, and engines rely on the following assumptions within their stated domain of validity.

Substrate Homogeneity

The causal propagation speed c is assumed constant unless substrate flow v_sub is explicitly modified.

Markov Property (Effective)

Effective engines assume:

x_{t+1} = f(x_t)


The next state depends only on the current state and drift dynamics.
No hidden memory beyond information density is assumed.

Stability via Saturation

The substrate possesses a physical ceiling, represented by saturation terms, preventing unbounded growth and structural collapse.

Pairwise Superposition Validity Limit

Pairwise superposition of substrate effects is assumed to hold only below multi-substrate excitation thresholds.

When three or more irreducible substrates are simultaneously active beyond a critical coupling point, additive approximations may fail.
In such regimes, interaction effects become non-pairwise and non-additive, and system behavior may transition into a new operational phase.

This is an effective limit, not a violation of the fundamental laws.

## 3. Emergent Operational Principles (Not Physical Laws)

The following principles describe observed system behavior under Phase-1 physics.
They are emergent, coarse-grained, and operational in nature.

Phase Stratification Principle

Substrate states occupy discrete operational regimes separated by critical excitation thresholds.
Phase labels (e.g., NOMINAL, TANGLE, FIREWALL) are coarse-grained classifications, not microscopic states.

Triadic Coupling Principle

When three irreducible substrates simultaneously constrain system evolution, conflict cannot be resolved pairwise.

In such regimes:

each substrate is constrained by the combined state of the other two,

no local adjustment reduces global tension,

instability becomes a configuration-level property rather than a local threshold crossing,

system tension may scale geometrically rather than arithmetically.

This principle explains the emergence of hidden instability regimes detected by higher-level engines.

Intervention Coupling Principle

External actions may temporarily modify transport coefficients or induce non-adiabatic state resets.
Such interventions are agent-mediated and break closed-system evolution.
